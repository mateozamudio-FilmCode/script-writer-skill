## POINT 1: THE PANDEMIC ($200K LESSON) [1:00 – 3:00]

Back in 2020, I was running an arbitrage model across apartments and houses — anywhere I could get them. We had no margin analysis. No unique value proposition. No product-market fit. I was just blindly following the trend. I didn't care about finding my own way because I saw companies like Sonder raising hundreds of millions doing exactly what I was doing.

Then Q1 2020 hit. The pandemic dropped our revenue to zero overnight. But all those leases were still due. 

I was staring at absolute negative cash flow on an arbitrage model with zero reserves. 

So I made a choice. I sold $200,000 of my personal assets to buy out every lease and make every landlord whole. While other operators essentially broke their leases and completely ghosted their landlords, I paid the fucking bill. 

[Stakes Check] 
If your business has no unique value proposition, no margin analysis, and you're just another copy-paste operation, you are the first to die when the market shifts. But those landlords I made right with? They became our ideal customer profile. They became the absolute foundation of the company I'm running now.

**You're either a defensible operator or a copy-paste operator. If your competitive advantage is "I have an Airbnb account," you're going to get slaughtered. That's some 2020 shit.**

[Cuts back to dry analysis]
Before you sign your next lease or take your next unit, look at the math. Do you have margin analysis? Do you know your exact breakeven? Do you have a value proposition that is measurably different from the 50 other operators in your market? 

If the answer to any of those is no, you're building exactly what I built. And I can tell you exactly how it ends. I paid for that lesson in ulcers.

## POINT 2: THE STUDY PERIOD (IGNORANCE DEBT) [3:00 – 4:30]

After the pandemic wiped the business, I had something I didn't want: time. No revenue to chase. And that came with a brutal realization — I didn't know what I didn't know. 

In order to be a successful entrepreneur, you have to humble yourself and realize you are actually the dumbest person in the room. No presumptions that you were born with the skill sets. That doesn't exist. 

So I started studying. Not casually scrolling TikTok for business hacks — obsessively studying. I found the people operating ten levels above me and studied them deeply. I watched Alex Hormozi's four-hour deconstraining workshop five times. I took notes. I layered Frank Slootman's principle that there is only one constraint at a time. 

**I thought being an entrepreneur meant taking meetings at SoHo house and pointing at whiteboards. The reality was me sitting in my basement wearing the same hoodie for three days, watching a four-hour video at 4:30 in the morning trying to figure out why I was so uniquely bad at business.**

The pandemic was a blessing in disguise because it took chasing money off the table and replaced it with understanding what it actually takes to become a great entrepreneur. 

You either burn years stumbling through trial and error, or you pay down your ignorance debt. If you're not studying, your ignorance debt is accruing. And the interest rate is brutal.

Who are you studying? Pick one operator, one framework, one system that is above where you are right now. Watch it until you can teach it. Model it against your own business.

## POINT 3: THE 25-PERSON TEAM ($500K CASH FLOW HOLE) [4:30 – 6:30]

Fast forward to Winter 2024. Cloud9 has a team of 25 people. I was hiring the highest positions I'd ever hired — head of marketing, head of sales, head of revenue. I assumed that senior titles meant senior capability. 

I was selling myself on what inputs I thought were important and working forwards, rather than looking at the outcome and working backwards.

My new head of revenue came from a hostel company and a discount airline. We run luxury short-term rentals in Chicago. It's a completely different product with completely different pricing science. He didn't know how to price luxury units. His methodology was: price high, drop repeatedly, and sell for peanuts. We were underperforming against a product that was twice as good as the market.


We had no pace reports. We had no forward booking tracking against budget. We didn't even have a system requiring it. We were flying absolutely blind into Chicago winter — the worst revenue season of the entire year.

Finance runs a cash flow projection at the end of December. Negative $500,000 for Q1 2025. In technical terms, we were actively paying people to sleep in our units. In non-technical terms, we were setting half a million dollars on fire and fanning the flames with our business plan.

I asked the revenue team what they were going to do about it. Their response was: "Let's figure it out next year. December is a festive month and we want to be with our families." 

[Beat]
And I realized — that work ethic? I'm the one who put it in the company. 

I was furious at them. But with some serious hard reflection, I realized I hired them. I put them in that position. I caused the negative half-million-dollar cash flow. The titles didn't fail. My architecture of the roles failed.

Understanding the difference between drivers and passengers — that was the clear moment. You are the bottleneck and your ego is the bodyguard.

It took six months to refocus. We cut the diluted skill. We reduced the workforce in quantity and started looking entirely for quality and specialization. Look at your team right now. For every person, can you name the specific problem they are solving? If you can't, you don't have a team. You have a payroll.

## POINT 4: THE KEY-MAN RISK (SECOND REVENUE MANAGER) [6:30 – 8:30]

In June 2025, I hired a new revenue manager to replace the first one. She delivered. Cloud9 closed 2025 at $8 million with 24 to 25% net margins. It was our first truly profitable year. 

I was on the absolute high of how much money we were making. 

Then, on December 25th — she disappears. Takes four weeks off. No warning. Everything was left on autopilot. Every system drops.

By February 1st, we parted ways. And that's when I discovered the real horror story. She had absolutely no SOPs documented. No systems built. No network brought to the company. She held a chief-level position and the only asset she ever created was her own indispensability.

You just lived through a head of revenue who produced no infrastructure. You told yourself you learned the lesson. So what safeguards did you put in place when you hired the second one? 

The honest answer is — I didn't. I was high on the most addictive thing on earth: revenue

Leverage and compounding have to be matched together. She produced leverage. We got great results. But nothing was compounding underneath it. Cuando she left, we didn't just lose an employee. We lost the entire revenue management capability of the company. 

But here is the build. Three weeks ago, we built a system. 

[B-ROLL: Screen recording of the PriceLabs dashboard and GitHub repo architecture]

It's a GitHub repo connected to Claude Code. We have skills that pull meeting recordings from Gmail, extract takeaways, and track every pricing change inside PriceLabs with timestamps. The system monitors pacing against changes, it measures pickup within the booking window, and when a strategy actually works — it gets named, assigned success criteria by unit type, and locked into the rules of the revenue management department. 

If it works, it gets locked in and repeated. If it fails, it's recorded so we never try it again. And the company owns all of it.



**This is the core of our "Never Naked Again" philosophy. No single person should ever leave my company without having documented their systems so completely that a golden retriever could take over their job tomorrow morning. The talent can leave; the asset stays.**

Open your business right now. If your best person quit tomorrow, what systems exist that would keep their function running? If the answer is nothing — you don't have a business. You have a collection of talented people doing you a favor.

## THE LESSON [8:30 – 9:00]

Every one of these lessons cost me real money, real time, and real sleepless nights. But the lesson underneath every single one of them is the same: if it's not documented, systemized, and owned by the company, it simply doesn't exist. Your edge isn't your own talent. Your edge isn't your team's talent. Your edge is the system that captures what works and makes it repeatable — whether you're there or not.

Which is a really easy thing to say in an edited YouTube video, and an incredibly hard thing to actually do at 2 AM when your revenue manager just ghosted and your PriceLabs dashboard looks like a crime scene.

It's the GitHub repo that doesn't delete itself when your team takes a vacation.
