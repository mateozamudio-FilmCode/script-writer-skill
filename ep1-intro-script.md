## MOMENT 1: THE PANDEMIC — $200K LESSON [1:30 – 3:15]

[looks at camera]

Let me take you back to early 2020.

Pre-pandemic, I had short-term rental units everywhere. Apartments, houses, spread across markets. Pure arbitrage model — landlords would rent to me as long as I had the cash. No margin analysis on individual units. No value proposition. No product-market fit. Just growth.

And my growth model? Based on Sonder — a company raising hundreds of millions of dollars that never became profitable.

I was following the trend. Finding my own way wasn't something I was interested in after seeing the hundreds of millions Sonder was raising. Why would I think differently? Then Q1 2020 hits. Pandemic.

Zero revenue. Every lease still due. Staring at absolute negative cash flow on an arbitrage model with no reserves. And the landlords — the ones who trusted me with their properties — they're calling.

Here's what happened next, and this is the part most people skip when they tell their pandemic story. Other operators walked away. Broke leases. Ghosted landlords. Disappeared.

I sold personal savings and assets — two hundred thousand dollars — to buy out every lease and make every landlord whole.

Two hundred grand. Gone.

## MOMENT 2: THE STUDY PERIOD — IGNORANCE DEBT [3:15 – 5:00]

After the pandemic wiped the business, I had something I didn't expect. Time.

No revenue to chase. No units to fill. And a question I didn't want to answer: do I even know how to run a real business, or did I just get lucky riding a wave that crashed?

You have to humble yourself and realize you don't know anything. You learn it — or you pay for not learning it. And the interest rate on that ignorance? It's your life.

So I started studying. Not casually — obsessively. My kitchen table at midnight, laptop open, same four-hour Hormozi workshop playing for the fifth time. Google Doc with notes that looked like a conspiracy theory — arrows, highlights, bolded questions I couldn't answer yet. Modeled different ways to deconstrain Cloud9 until the real constraint surfaced. Layered Slootman's principle: there's only one constraint at a time. Layered Musk's principle: there are no rules — throw everything at the constraint until it breaks.

You know what people think a CEO does at 11 PM? Strategy calls. Investor dinners. Shit that sounds important. I was rewinding the same YouTube video for the fifth time with a Google Doc that looked like a conspiracy theorist's manifesto. That's the entrepreneur lifestyle nobody posts about.

Hormozi's implementation workshop — four and a half hours. I watched it in September. Again in October. Twice in December. And the third time through, it hit me: our constraint wasn't more units. It wasn't more bookings. It was getting more revenue-share buildings from owners who have a specific pain point we actually solve. That's a constraint nobody in this industry has truly identified — because everyone's chasing volume instead of fit.

The following week I launched an internal project. All marketing and sales hands on deck. Three to four hours a day of my own time. We will not stop until that constraint is broken. That's what studying looks like when it actually works — it doesn't make you feel smart. It makes you realize you've been solving the wrong problem.

Who are you studying? Not scrolling — studying. Pick one operator, one framework, one system that's above where you are right now. Watch it until you can teach it. Model it against your own business. That's how declarative knowledge becomes procedural. That's how knowing becomes doing. If you're not doing that, your ignorance debt is accruing. And trust me — the interest rate is brutal.

## MOMENT 3: THE 25-PERSON TEAM — $500K CASH FLOW HOLE [5:00 – 6:45]

[shifts in chair]

Winter 2024. I had a team of twenty-five people. I was hiring the highest positions I'd ever hired — head of marketing, head of sales, head of revenue. And I made an assumption that nearly killed the company.

I assumed that senior titles meant senior capability.

I was selling myself on what inputs I thought were important and working forwards rather than working backwards. I hired a head of revenue who came from a hostel company and a discount airline. My product? Luxury short-term rentals in Chicago. Completely different product. Completely different pricing science. He didn't know how to price luxury units.

His methodology: price high, drop repeatedly, sell for peanuts. Annual occupancy in the low fifties, high forties. Weekday underperformance against a product that was twice as good as anything else on the market.

No pace reports. No forward booking tracking against budget. No system requiring any of it.

Flying blind into Chicago winter — the worst revenue season of the year. That's like flying a plane with no instruments into a mountain range and saying "I'm sure the weather will work out." Spoiler: the weather did not work out.

Finance team runs a cash flow projection end of December. Negative five hundred thousand dollars for Q1 2025.

[beat]

I asked the revenue team what they planned to do about it. Their response — and I want you to hear this exactly — "Let's figure it out next year. December is a festive month and we want to be with our families."

[looks directly at camera]

And I realized — that shit? That "let's deal with it next year" energy? I'm the one who fucking put it in the company.

I was upset at them. Furious, actually. But with some serious hard reflection, I realized I'm the one who hired them. I'm the one who put them in that position. And I'm the one who caused the negative half-million-dollar cash flow. The titles didn't fail. I failed. My architecture of those roles was horseshit and I was too proud to see it.

You are the bottleneck and your ego is the bodyguard. Understanding the difference between drivers and passengers — that was the clear moment.

It took six months to refocus. Cut the diluted skill. Reduced workforce in quantity. Started looking for quality and specialization. Stopped hiring titles and started hiring for specific problems.

Look at your team right now. For every person on your payroll, can you name the specific problem they solve? Not their title — the problem. If you can't, you don't have a team. You have a payroll.
