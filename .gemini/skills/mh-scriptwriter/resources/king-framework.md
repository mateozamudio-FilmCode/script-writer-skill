## 1. Core Philosophy

Writing is telepathy. The writer transmits a clear image from their mind into the reader's mind using words. Every craft decision — word choice, structure, pacing — either sharpens or blurs that transmission.

Stories are not manufactured. They are pre-existing relics (King calls them "fossils") that the writer excavates. The writer's job is careful extraction, not invention. The material controls the writer, not the other way around.

**Audit implication:** When a piece of content feels forced or overstructured, it might be because the writer is manufacturing plot instead of following the story's natural shape. Look for signs of the writer fighting the material.

## 2. The Toolbox

The mental carrying case of skills every writer needs. Organized in levels:

### Level 1: Bread and Butter

**Vocabulary:** Use the first word that comes to mind. Never search for a longer, more impressive word to dress up prose. The right word is almost always the simple one. If the writer reaches for "utilize" when "use" works, that's a flag.

**Grammar:** Rely on simple noun-verb constructions. The subject does the action. Complexity in grammar should serve complexity in meaning — never complexity for its own sake.

**Passive Voice:** The subject of the sentence must always be doing the action.
- Passive: "The meeting was called by the CEO" → Active: "The CEO called the meeting"
- Passive: "Mistakes were made" → Active: "I made mistakes"
- Passive voice hides the actor, drains energy, and makes prose feel institutional rather than human

**Adverbs:** Not your friend. Especially in dialogue attribution. The adverb is a symptom of a weak verb.
- "He ran quickly" → "He sprinted"
- "She said quietly" → "She whispered"
- "He closed the door firmly" → "He slammed the door"
- Adverbs in dialogue tags are the worst: "he said menacingly" tells the reader how to feel instead of letting the dialogue do its job

### Level 2: Form and Style

**The Paragraph:** The paragraph — not the sentence — is the basic unit of fiction and narrative nonfiction. It controls the rhythm and pacing of the story. Short paragraphs = speed. Long paragraphs = depth or slowing. The writer controls the audience's reading speed through paragraph length.

**Description:** A few well-chosen details trigger the reader's imagination more effectively than exhaustive description. Don't describe everything about a person's appearance — pick the one or two details that define them. Let the reader's imagination do the rest. Over-description is a trust problem: the writer doesn't trust the reader to build the movie.

## 3. The 10% Cut Rule

**Second Draft = First Draft – 10%**

Every piece of writing has bloat. The revision process is fundamentally about removal. Calculate 10% of the total word count — that's your minimum cut target.

**What to cut:**
- Filler words and phrases: "basically," "actually," "really," "very," "in order to," "the fact that," "it is important to note that," "what I mean by this is"
- Repeated ideas restated in different words (once is teaching; twice is insecure; three times is insulting)
- Throat-clearing: sentences that exist to get the writer warmed up but add nothing for the reader
- Unnecessary qualifiers: "somewhat," "a little bit," "kind of," "sort of"
- Redundant pairs: "each and every," "first and foremost," "null and void"
- Unnecessary backstory that doesn't serve the current scene
- Transitional scaffolding the reader doesn't need: "Now let me turn to..." "Moving on..."

**What to compress:**
- Timelines that can be collapsed without losing meaning
- Sequences of events where the audience only needs the first and last
- Extended dialogue that can be summarized except for the key lines
- Descriptions of routine actions (getting ready, traveling, etc.) unless they carry thematic weight

**Audit application:** Calculate the current word count. Identify specific passages that contribute to bloat. Estimate a target cut. The 10% is a minimum — some pieces need 20-30%.

## 4. The Ideal Reader

The Ideal Reader (I.R.) is a single specific person the writer imagines as their audience. Every decision about pacing, clarity, and emotional impact is filtered through: "Would my I.R. stay engaged here? Would they be confused? Bored? Moved?"

**Audit application:** When auditing content, simulate the Ideal Reader persona. At each section, ask:

- **Boredom risk:** Would the I.R. skim this? If yes, it needs cutting or restructuring.
- **Confusion risk:** Would the I.R. need to re-read this? If yes, it needs clarity.
- **Narrative drag:** Would the I.R. feel the story has stalled? If yes, the momentum is broken.
- **Over-explanation:** Would the I.R. think "I already got it"? If yes, the writer doesn't trust their audience.

The I.R. is smart, attentive, but has no obligation to stay. If the content loses them, they leave.

## 5. Situation Over Plot

King argues that plotting — imposing a predetermined structure on the material — breaks the fossil. Instead, rely on *situation*: put characters in a compelling "what if" predicament and watch them try to escape.

**Audit implication:** When a piece of content feels mechanical or predictable, it might be overplotted. The beats feel like checkboxes rather than organic developments. Look for:
- Events that serve the structure but feel unmotivated by character
- Twists that feel engineered rather than earned
- An ending that feels predetermined rather than inevitable-in-retrospect

This doesn't mean structure is bad — it means structure should emerge from the characters and their situation, not be imposed from above.

## 6. Show, Don't Tell

Never dictate a character's trait or emotional state directly if you can reveal it through dialogue, action, or specific detail.

**Telling:** "Marcus was nervous about the meeting."
**Showing:** "Marcus checked his phone three times in the elevator. He straightened his tie, then loosened it, then straightened it again."

**Telling:** "The company was in trouble."
**Showing:** "The parking lot was half empty at 9 AM on a Tuesday. The coffee machine in the break room had a sign: 'Out of Order — No ETA.'"

**Audit application:** Flag every instance where the writer tells the audience what to feel or think instead of creating the conditions for them to feel or think it themselves. "I was devastated" is telling. The specific details that convey devastation are showing.

---

## 7. Backstory Management

**Backstory stays back.** Research, character history, and contextual information are only useful for creating verisimilitude — the feeling of reality. They should remain in the background, woven into the story through small details and implications, never delivered as exposition dumps.

**Audit application:** Flag any section where the story pauses its forward momentum to deliver background information. Ask: can this information be woven into action, dialogue, or a single telling detail instead? If the backstory is essential, can it be delivered in 1-2 sentences instead of a paragraph?

**The iceberg principle:** The audience should sense there's more beneath the surface without the writer showing them everything. The unseen depth creates the feeling of reality. The visible surface is what they need to follow the story.

## 8. Line-Level Mechanics

### Sentence Construction
- Subject → Verb → Object is the foundation
- Vary sentence length for rhythm (short sentences for impact, longer for flow)
- The most important word in a sentence should go at the end
- The first and last sentences of a paragraph carry the most weight

### Word Selection Hierarchy
1. **Strong nouns** — specific, concrete, visual (not "vehicle" but "pickup truck"; not "building" but "courthouse")
2. **Active verbs** — carrying the action without modification (not "moved quickly" but "darted"; not "said loudly" but "shouted")
3. **Sensory details** — one or two per scene, not an inventory
4. **Adjectives** — sparingly, when the noun alone can't carry the meaning
5. **Adverbs** — almost never

### Dialogue Rules
- Default attribution: "said" / "asked"
- Avoid creative attribution: "exclaimed," "pronounced," "interjected," "opined"
- Avoid adverb-modified attribution: "said softly," "said angrily," "said nervously"
- The dialogue itself should convey the tone; the attribution is invisible scaffolding
- Characters must sound distinct — different vocabulary, rhythm, sentence length

## 9. Revision Process

King's revision sequence (adapted for auditing):

1. **Read in one sitting** — experience the piece as the audience will, not as the writer did
2. **Fix housekeeping** — plot holes, inconsistencies, factual errors
3. **Identify the theme** — what is this piece actually about? (The writer often discovers this in revision, not in drafting)
4. **Apply the 10% cut** — remove bloat, filler, repetition
5. **Enhance meaning** — strengthen the theme through selective revision
6. **Get feedback** — if reviewers are divided, ignore it (tie goes to the writer). If all reviewers agree something is broken, fix it.

---

## 10. Audit Application Rules

When using this framework in the story auditor:

1. **Passive voice audit:** Flag every instance. Provide the active alternative. Count total instances as a percentage of total sentences — above 10% is a serious problem.

2. **Adverb audit:** Flag every adverb (especially in dialogue attribution). Provide a stronger verb replacement. Count total adverbs per 1000 words — above 5 per 1000 is excessive.

3. **Bloat identification:** Calculate current word count, identify the 10% cut target, and list specific passages that could be cut or compressed. Don't just say "cut this" — explain why it's bloat (filler, repetition, unnecessary backstory, etc.).

4. **Cinema check (complementary to Dicks):** Every scene needs a physical location. King's version: every scene needs enough specific detail for the reader to build the movie. Not exhaustive description — just the right two or three details.

5. **Show vs. Tell audit:** Flag every instance where the writer tells instead of shows. Provide a "showing" alternative where possible.

6. **Ideal Reader simulation:** At each major section, note the boredom/confusion/drag risk. This is subjective but critical — it's the gut-level reading experience audit.
