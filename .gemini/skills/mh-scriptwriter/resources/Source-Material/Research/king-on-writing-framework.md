# Stephen King — *On Writing* Framework
## Extracted framework for writing craft reference
### Source: *On Writing: A Memoir of the Craft* by Stephen King

---

## I. Central Idea and Thesis

- **The Thesis:** Good writing is a learned skill that consists of mastering basic fundamental tools and practicing relentlessly.
- **The Limitation:** You cannot make a bad writer competent, and you cannot make a good writer great. However, with hard work and dedication, you can make a competent writer good.
- **The Core Mechanism:** Writing is **telepathy**. The writer's goal is to transmit a clear image from their mind directly into the reader's mind using words.

---

## II. Key Definitions and Terms

- **The Fossil:** The story itself. Stories are not manufactured things; they are pre-existing, undiscovered relics buried in the ground. The writer's job is to excavate them carefully.
- **The Muse:** The creative subconscious. It is not a magical fairy, but a hardheaded "basement guy" who only provides inspiration if you put in the physical grunt work on a strict daily schedule.
- **Ideal Reader (I.R.):** The single specific person you imagine writing for. You use the I.R. to gauge your story's pacing, clarity, and emotional impact.
- **The Toolbox:** The mental carrying case of skills a writer must build to avoid getting discouraged by difficult writing tasks.

---

## III. Supporting Sub-Framework: The Writer's Toolbox

The foundational skills required to write fiction.

### Level 1: The Bread and Butter

- **Vocabulary:** Pack the words you already know. Use the very first word that comes to your mind. Never intentionally search for long, complex words to dress up your prose.
- **Grammar:** Rely on simple Noun-Verb constructions.
- **The Passive Voice:** Avoid it completely. The subject of the sentence must always be doing the action, never having the action done to it.
- **Adverbs:** The adverb is not your friend. Ruthlessly eliminate them, especially in dialogue attribution. Always use "he said" or "she said" instead of modifying the speech.

### Level 2: Form and Style

- **The Paragraph:** The paragraph, not the sentence, is the true basic unit of fiction. It is the beat and rhythm of the story.
- **Description:** Use a few well-chosen details to trigger the reader's imagination. Do not over-describe physical appearances or clothing.

---

## IV. Mental Models and Rules for Decision-Making

- **Situation Over Plot:** Plotting is an anti-creative jackhammer that breaks the fossil. Instead, rely on **Situation**. Put characters in a "What-if" predicament, watch them try to escape, and transcribe what happens naturally.
- **Absolute Honesty:** You must tell the truth about how people act and talk. Do not censor character dialogue or behavior to appease polite society or moral critics.
- **The 10% Cut Rule:** **2nd Draft = 1st Draft – 10%**. Every story has bloat. You must remove at least 10% of the word count in the revision.
- **Back Story Stays Back:** Research and character history are only useful for verisimilitude. Keep them strictly in the background so they do not slow the pace of the main story.
- **Show, Don't Tell:** Never dictate a character's trait directly to the reader if you can reveal it through their dialogue or actions.

---

## V. Conditions, Constraints, and Assumptions

- **The Prerequisite:** If you do not have the time to read, you do not have the tools or the time to write.
- **The Attitude Constraint:** You can approach writing with any emotion, but you must **never come to the blank page lightly**.
- **The Story is the Boss:** The writer does not control the material; the material controls the writer.

---

## VI. Practical Application Sequence: The Step-by-Step Method

### Phase 1: The Inputs (Preparation)

1. **Read Constantly:** Read bad prose to learn what to avoid. Read great prose to measure your skills and learn style. Read in sips (waiting rooms, treadmills) instead of just long sessions.
2. **Eliminate Distractions:** Turn off the television. Create a private workspace with a door you can physically close.
3. **Set a Daily Goal:** Establish a strict daily word count (e.g., 1,000 to 2,000 words a day).

### Phase 2: The First Draft (Door Closed)

4. **Write Fast:** Download the story directly from your head to the page as fast as possible to outrun self-doubt.
5. **Stay Isolated:** Write the first draft purely for yourself. Do not show it to anyone, do not ask for feedback, and do not let the outside world in.
6. **Ignore Theme:** Do not worry about symbolism or underlying meaning during this draft. Focus purely on unearthing the story.

### Phase 3: The Incubation Period (Rest)

7. **Lock it Away:** Once the draft is done, put it in a drawer for a minimum of six weeks.
8. **Clear the Mind:** Do not look at the manuscript. Work on something entirely different to recycle your creative imagination.

### Phase 4: The Review (Analysis)

9. **Read in One Sitting:** Take the manuscript out and read it back as if it were written by someone else.
10. **Fix Housekeeping:** Correct glaring plot holes, misspellings, and character inconsistencies.
11. **Identify the Theme:** Ask yourself what the story is actually about. Identify recurring elements, symbols, and the central theme.

### Phase 5: The Second Draft (Door Open)

12. **Let the World In:** Give the manuscript to your Ideal Reader and 4 to 8 trusted peers.
13. **Enhance the Meaning:** Rewrite to clarify and strengthen the theme and symbolism you discovered in the review phase.
14. **Prune the Pace:** Apply the 10% reduction formula to remove boring parts, excess adverbs, and unneeded back story.
15. **Apply Feedback:** If reviewers are divided on a critique, ignore it (tie goes to the writer). If all reviewers agree something is broken, you must fix it.
