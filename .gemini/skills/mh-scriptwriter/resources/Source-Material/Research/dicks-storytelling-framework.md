# Matthew Dicks — Storytelling Framework
## Extracted framework for storytelling craft reference
### Source: Matthew Dicks's storytelling system (*Storyworthy* and related work)

---

## I. Central Idea and Thesis

- **The Thesis:** Storytelling is the most powerful tool for human connection. Great stories do not require extraordinary circumstances; they are built from small, everyday moments of realization or transformation.
- **The Core Mechanism:** Every great story is ultimately about a single "five-second moment" in a person's life. The sole purpose of the story is to bring that five-second moment to the greatest clarity possible.

---

## II. Key Definitions and Terms

- **The Five-Second Moment:** A fleeting moment of fundamental change, realization, or transformation in the storyteller's life.
- **The Dinner Test:** The rule that a story told on a stage or in a boardroom should sound similar to the version you would tell friends over dinner. No theatrical voices or artificial gestures.
- **Cinema of the Mind:** The necessity of providing a physical location for every scene so the audience's mental movie never stops running.
- **The Elephant:** A clear statement of the need, want, problem, peril, or mystery established within the first minute of the story.
- **Backpacks:** Loading the audience with your specific hopes, fears, and plans right before a major event to increase their anticipation.
- **Breadcrumbs:** Hinting at a future event while strategically withholding the specifics to keep the audience guessing.
- **Hourglasses:** Intentionally dragging out the wait and slowing the pacing down right before a major reveal to maximize suspense.
- **Crystal Balls:** A false but highly plausible prediction made by the storyteller to make the audience wonder if it will come true.

---

## III. Primary Principles and Pillars

- **The Change Principle:** Every story must reflect change over time. If you do not start in one state of being and end in another, it is merely an anecdote, not a story.
- **The Protagonist Rule:** You must tell your own story. Even if the story involves others, you must tell your side of the story and act as the protagonist.
- **The But/Therefore Principle:** Causal linkage is the engine of a story. Scenes and sentences must be connected by "but" (signaling a shift) or "therefore" (signaling causation). Never connect events with just the word "and".
- **The Present Tense Rule:** Tell the main action of the story in the present tense to create visceral immediacy. Shift to the past tense only for backstory.

---

## IV. Cause-and-Effect Logic

- **If** you record small daily moments, **then** you will develop a "storytelling lens" that makes you perceive your life as vast, rich, and highly meaningful.
- **If** you remove all thesis statements and summaries from your story's opening, **then** you preserve the element of surprise, which is the only way to trigger an emotional response from the audience.

---

## V. Inputs, Actions, and Outcomes

- **Inputs:** Five minutes of daily reflection, pen, and a two-column spreadsheet.
- **Actions:** Identifying the most story-worthy moment of every day, mining past memories without judgment, and linking events causally.
- **Outcomes:** A permanent, growing repository of stories, a slower subjective experience of time, and the ability to hold an audience's attention.

---

## VI. Practical Application Sequence: The Step-by-Step Method

### Phase 1: Harvest the Content

1. **Do Homework for Life:** At the end of every day, ask yourself what the most story-worthy moment was. Write it down in 1-3 sentences.
2. **Generate Memories:** Use Crash & Burn (stream-of-consciousness writing) or First/Last/Best/Worst grids to unearth forgotten life events.

### Phase 2: Build the Frame

3. **Identify the Ending:** Pinpoint your five-second moment of change. Distill it into a single sentence. Everything in the story must serve this moment.
4. **Find the Beginning:** Start the story at the exact opposite emotional state of your five-second moment to create a narrative arc (e.g., if ending in love, start in disgust).
5. **Start Late:** Push the beginning as close to the climax as physically possible to cut bloat.
6. **Open with Action:** Start with physical forward movement. Never open with a summary or expectation-setting statement.

### Phase 3: Craft the Middle

7. **Introduce the Elephant:** State the stakes or problem in the first 30 to 60 seconds.
8. **Link with Causation:** Connect every scene using "but" or "therefore".
9. **Maintain the Cinema:** Assign a physical location to every thought, conversation, and backstory so the audience's mental movie never stops.
10. **Build Suspense:** Deploy Backpacks, Breadcrumbs, and Hourglasses to make the audience desperately want to hear the next sentence.

### Phase 4: Polish and Perform

11. **Apply Permissible Lies:** Simplify reality. Omit unnecessary people, compress time, and conflate emotions to make the narrative clean.
12. **Memorize Scenes, Not Words:** Memorize the first lines, the last lines, and the physical locations (scenes) of the story. Do not memorize it word-for-word.

## VII. Mental Models and Rules for Decision-Making

- **The "Power of the Negative" Rule:** Stating what something *is not* is far more engaging than stating what it *is*. It creates a binary choice and gives the sentence momentum.
- **The Success Story Rule:** To avoid sounding arrogant when telling a story about a victory, you must malign yourself early in the story and marginalize your accomplishment (focus on small steps, not massive leaps).
- **End on Heart:** Use humor in the middle to maintain engagement, but never end a story on a joke. End on meaning and vulnerability.
- **The Permissible Lies:** You cannot invent facts, but you *must* manipulate reality for the audience's benefit. You may omit boring facts, compress timelines, assume forgotten minor details, reorder events for emotional trajectory, and conflate slow emotional changes into sudden realizations.

---

## VIII. Supporting Sub-Frameworks

### The Crash & Burn Framework

Set a timer for 10-15 minutes. Write without stopping.
- **Rule 1:** Do not get attached to any idea; let new thoughts interrupt.
- **Rule 2:** Do not judge or correct grammar.
- **Rule 3:** Never let the pen stop moving.

### Humor Generation Models

- **Milk Cans & Baseball:** Build a long list of setups (milk cans) and drop the funniest, most surprising word at the absolute end of the sentence (the baseball).
- **Babies & Blenders:** Create a list of three items where the third is wildly incongruous with the first two.

---

## IX. Conditions, Constraints, and Assumptions

- **The Consistency Constraint:** Finding stories requires absolute daily commitment to the "Homework for Life" exercise. You must not skip days.
- **The Mirror Constraint:** Never practice a story in front of a mirror. You will never look at yourself while performing, so practicing this way builds false awareness.
- **The Prop Constraint:** Never use props. They instantly destroy the "cinema of the mind" and remind the audience they are just watching a person on a stage.
- **The Constraint of Reality:** You can omit and compress, but you can never add a person or event to a story that did not actually exist.
