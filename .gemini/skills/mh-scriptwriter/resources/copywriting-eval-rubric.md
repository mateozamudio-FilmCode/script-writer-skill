# Evaluation Rubric: AI Copywriting Skill (The Operational Realist)

This rubric is designed to evaluate AI-generated scripts for "The Operational Realist" brand. It focuses on balancing technical "operational math" with a specific style of humor—the **Hummiston Method**—where the joke is a byproduct of the serious content.

---

## 📊 Evaluation Criteria

### 1. Humor Integration (The Hummiston/Hormozi Standard)
**Goal:** Jokes must be "load-bearing." They should use the serious setup as the punchline rather than stopping the video to "tell a joke."

* **Score 5 (Master):** Humor arises from situational irony or self-deprecation regarding the data. The seriousness of the setup creates the "snap" of the punchline. (e.g., Mocking the "entrepreneur lifestyle" while staring at a "crime scene" dashboard).
* **Score 3 (Average):** A joke is present, but it feels like a "Humor Gap"—a temporary break from the content rather than a subversion of it.
* **Score 1 (Fail):** The humor is generic, unrelated to STR/Operations, or uses "dad jokes" that break the "Operational Realist" authority.

### 2. Expression Economy & Brand "Lore"
**Goal:** Prevent brand slogans like "Tuition Paid" or "Never Naked Again" from becoming repetitive filler or confusing jargon.

* **Score 5:** Brand expressions are used once at the moment of highest emotional impact. Proprietary terms are defined *before* they are used as a mantra.
* **Score 3:** Expression is used 2–3 times, losing its "punch." The concept is named, but the definition is buried in a wall of text.
* **Score 1:** The script relies on the catchphrase as a crutch. Uses "Internal Lore" (e.g., private logs) without explaining what they mean to a first-time viewer.

### 3. Rhythmic Flow (The Rule of 3s)
**Goal:** The script should feel like a late-night strategy session between peers, not a keynote speech.

* **Score 5:** Frequent use of the **Rule of Three** for punchy delivery. Transitions between moments feel like a natural progression of a single narrative.
* **Score 3:** The sections are distinct but feel like a "listicle" (Step 1, Step 2, Step 3) rather than a conversation.
* **Score 1:** Heavy use of "Corporate Speak" or overly formal transitions (e.g., "In conclusion," "Moving on to my next point").

### 4. Procedural vs. Declarative Knowledge
**Goal:** Move from "I learned this philosophy" to "I did X on Monday because of it."

* **Score 5:** Includes a specific "Procedural Moment"—a concrete decision, tool change, or data point (e.g., per-unit margin, breakeven occupancy) that resulted from the lesson.
* **Score 3:** Describes the "learning process" (studying Hormozi/Slootman) but lacks the specific "Monday Morning" application.
* **Score 1:** Stays purely philosophical (e.g., "You must be humble") without any data or operational backing.

---

## 🤡 The "Ryan Hummiston" Humor Checklist
*Before finalizing a script, check the jokes against these three rules:*

1.  **Is it situational?** Does it reference the "weather" of the business (e.g., PriceLabs, pace reports, or cash flow projections)?
2.  **Does the seriousness set it up?** Is the joke a subversion of a high-stakes moment?
3.  **The "Abrupt Snap":** Does the joke end quickly to pivot back to a cold, hard operational truth?

> **Example of a "Hummiston" Success:**
> Talking about vitamin D while walking outside in a clearly cloudy day
> "…Also the fact that you can synthetize it yourself by going outside.. Except that is been raining all day and this was a stupid stupid example"

---

## 📈 Final Scoring
* **18–20 Points:** **Elite.** Ready for filming.
* **14–17 Points:** **Strong.** Needs a "Humor Pass" to ensure jokes are load-bearing.
* **Under 14 Points:** **Rewrite.** Content is likely too "keynote" or lacks the "Operational Realist" edge.
