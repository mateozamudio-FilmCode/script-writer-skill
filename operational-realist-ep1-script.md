## 4. MOMENT 1: The Pandemic ($200K Lesson) [1:30 – 3:00]

Let's jump back to pre-pandemic. Short-term rental units. Apartments, houses, everywhere and anywhere. The arbitrage model. Landlords would rent to me as long as I had the cash. We had absolutely no margin analysis on individual units. No value proposition. No product-market fit. 

Our growth model was based on Sonder — a company raising hundreds of millions that never became profitable. I was following the trend. Finding my own way wasn't something I was interested in after seeing the hundreds of millions Sonder was raising.

Then Q1 2020 hits. Pandemic. Zero revenue. All leases still due. Staring at absolute negative cash flow on an arbitrage model with no reserves.

I sold my personal savings and assets. Two hundred thousand dollars. To buy out every lease and make every landlord whole. Other operators walked away, broke leases, ghosted landlords. I chose to honor the contracts.

## 5. MOMENT 2: The Study Period (Ignorance Debt) [3:00 – 4:30]

After the pandemic wiped the business, I had something I didn't want: time. No revenue to chase. And an absolutely brutal realization: I didn't know what I didn't know.

In order to be a successful entrepreneur, you have to humble yourself and realize you don't know anything. No presumptions that you were born with the skill sets. That just doesn't exist.

So I started studying the greats. Not casually. Obsessively. I found Alex Hormozi's four-hour deconstraining workshop and watched it three, maybe five times. I took notes. I modeled different ways to deconstrain Cloud9 until the real constraint surfaced. I layered Frank Slootman's principle that there's only one constraint at a time. I layered Elon Musk's principle to throw everything at the constraint to break it.

The pandemic was a blessing in disguise because it took chasing money off the table and replaced it with understanding what it actually takes to build a defensible asset.

**Everyone thinks the entrepreneur lifestyle is popping bottles on yachts, but the reality is sitting alone in a dark room watching the same four-hour video for the fifth time while questioning your own basic intelligence.** 
**But here is the reality:** you either burn years stumbling through trial and error, or you pay down your ignorance debt.

Ignorance debt is the cost of what you don't know, and it's accruing every day. The interest rate is your life.

Who are you studying? Not scrolling. Studying. Pick one operator, one framework, one system that's above where you are right now. Watch it until you can teach it. Model it against your own business. 

That following Monday after watching Slootman? I killed three software subscriptions, fired a consultant who was producing air, and refocused 100% of my time entirely on our pricing algorithm constraint. That's how declarative knowledge becomes procedural.

## 6. MOMENT 3: The 25-Person Team ($500K Cash Flow Hole) [4:30 – 6:00]

Fast forward a few years to Winter 2024. Team of 25 people. I was hiring the highest positions I'd ever hired — head of marketing, head of sales, head of revenue. I assumed that senior titles meant senior capability.

I was selling myself on what inputs I thought were important and working forwards rather than working backwards.

The head of revenue came from a hostel company and a discount airline. We run luxury short-term rentals in Chicago. Completely different product, completely different pricing science. He didn't know how to price luxury units. His methodology? Price high, drop repeatedly, sell for peanuts. Annual occupancy was in the low 50s, high 40s. Weekday underperformance against a product that was twice as good as the market.

We had no pace reports. No forward booking tracking against budget. No system requiring it from him. We were flying completely blind into Chicago winter — the absolute worst revenue season of the year.

My finance team runs a cash flow projection end of December. The number? Negative $500,000 for Q1 2025.

I sat down with the revenue team. I asked them what they were going to do about it. Their response?
"Let's figure it out next year. December is a festive month and we want to be with our families."

And I realized — that work ethic? I'm the one who put it in the company.

I was pissed at them. But with some serious hard reflection, I realized I'm the one who hired them, I'm the one who put them in that position, and I'm the absolute one who caused the negative half-million-dollar cash flow hole. The titles didn't fail. My architecture of the roles failed.

Understanding the difference between drivers and passengers — that was the clear moment.
You are the bottleneck and your ego is the bodyguard.

It took me six months to refocus. I cut the diluted skill. I reduced workforce in quantity and started looking for quality and specialization.

Look at your team right now. For every single person, can you name the specific problem they are solving? Not their title. The exact problem. If you can't, you don't have a team. You have a payroll.

## 7. MOMENT 4: The Key-Man Risk [6:00 – 7:30]

June 2025 comes around. I hired a new revenue manager to replace the first one. She delivered. Cloud9 closed 2025 at $8 million with 24 to 25% net margins. Our first truly profitable year.

I was riding the high of how much money we were making.

Then, December 25th hits over the holidays. She disappears. Four weeks. No warning at all. Everything goes on autopilot and all the systems drop. By February 1st, we parted ways.

That's when I discover the real damage. No SOPs documented. No systems built. No network brought to the company. She held a chief-level position and the only asset she created was her own indispensability.

That is the "misarchitected hire." Back then, "chief-level" meant they had a fancy resume and demanded a high salary. Today, "chief-level" means they build systems that outlive their tenure.

You just lived through a head of revenue who produced no infrastructure. You said you learned the lesson. So what safeguards did you put in place when you hired the second one? The brutal answer is — absolutely none. I was too high on the revenue to check the basement.

Leverage and compounding have to be matched. She was producing leverage — great results — but nothing was compounding underneath. No documented systems, no repeatable frameworks, no institutional knowledge. When she left, we didn't just lose an employee. We lost the entire revenue management capability of the company.

Three weeks ago, I decided that would never happen again. I built a system.

The system monitors pacing against changes, measures pickup within the booking window, and when a strategy works... it gets named, given success criteria by unit type, and locked into the rules of the revenue management department.

Now, to be clear, this system is only three weeks old. I'm not showing you a finished product from the mountaintop. I'm showing you what it looks like from the middle of the climb, when you actually start building differently.

If it works, it gets locked in and repeated. If it didn't work, it's recorded so we never try it again. And the company owns all of it.

You want every person on your payroll building something that stays when they leave. That is exactly what it means to be "Never Naked Again".

Open your business right now. If your best person quit tomorrow, what systems exist that would keep their function running? If the answer is nothing — you don't have a business. You have a collection of talented people doing you a favor.
