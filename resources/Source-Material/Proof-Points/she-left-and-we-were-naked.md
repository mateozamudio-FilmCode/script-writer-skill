# PRODUCTION-READY OUTLINE

# She Increased Revenue 30%. Then She Left. And We Were Naked.
### The Role Architecture Failure & The "Never Naked Again" Protocol

---

**Series:** Talking to Myself
**Format:** 5–10 minute YouTube episode
**Target Length:** 7–8 minutes
**Content Pillar:** Hiring & Delegation Science
**Status:** Documenting from inside the fire — vlog footage being collected now. This outline will be finalized once the rebuild is complete and a new revenue manager is in place.

> **THESIS:** She wasn't a bad hire. She was a misarchitected hire. The skill set worked. The revenue management produced results. What failed was everything around it — the title, the expectations, the documentation, the accountability structure, the exit protocol. A good operator in the wrong container. And when she left, everything she built walked out with her because none of it was captured.

## 3. POINT 1 — The Role Architecture Failure

**Framework:** Tasks vs. Systems Separation
**Timing:** 90–120 seconds

**Core Argument:**

Revenue managers execute within systems. CROs build systems. Marcus hired an executor for an architect's job — then blamed the executor when systems didn't get built.

**Key Lines from Interview:**

> "Every specific role has a specific set of problems that a proven skill set is designed to solve. If you don't research what the role actually requires, you're assuming one can do the next. The mistake was assigning a job title without understanding what the responsibilities are, what the skill sets are, what problems they're going to solve, and how those attribute to growth."

> "I gave somebody a position that I'm not ready to lead over because I don't know anything about it. Two big mistakes: hiring for a role I hadn't defined, and accepting accountability for a position I couldn't manage."

**The Accountability Flip (strongest moment):**

> "Rather than bringing in someone of that stature to take weight off my shoulders, I was allowing them to put more on. 'Give me ABC before I can do my job.' And I accepted that. I became the support system for the person who was supposed to be building my systems."

**Application for the Operator:**

Before you post any role, separate two lists: (1) Tasks they will DO — daily/weekly execution within existing systems. (2) Systems they will BUILD — infrastructure, SOPs, automations, dashboards. If you can't fill out both lists with specific deliverables and timelines, you're not ready to hire.

🎬 **B-ROLL:** Whiteboard or on-screen graphic: two columns — "Tasks They DO" vs. "Systems They BUILD." Revenue Manager sits in column 1. CRO sits in column 2. Simple, visual.

⚠️ **FLAG:** Marcus's answer about "when he realized" was initially split between "instantly" and "at three months." In the video, lean into the honest version: "I had a gut feeling at the negotiation table and I ignored it for three months because the revenue numbers were acting as a painkiller." Don't clean this up. The contradiction IS the lesson.

## 4. POINT 2 — The Institutional Knowledge Bleed

**Framework:** Exit Extraction Protocol
**Timing:** 90–120 seconds

**Core Argument:**

When someone leaves your business, you don't just lose a person — you lose undocumented institutional knowledge. If your business can't survive someone walking out the door, you don't have a business. You have a dependency.

**Key Lines from Interview:**

> "We run our business on daily pricing movement. Three to four hours a day looking at pickup, strategizing against high-demand days, pushing pricing to the max, closing in on booking windows. All of that knowledge was in her head. The day she left, she walked out with it."

> "We were naked."

**The Real Cost — Make It Specific:**

February budget: $370K. Currently at $280K with $90K to close. That's manageable in low season. But July and August budget: $1.2 million. If the pricing logic, booking window strategy, and demand forecasting aren't rebuilt and documented before high season, the exposure is massive.

**Application for the Operator:**

Build an Exit Extraction Protocol into every role from day one. This means: every system your hire touches must produce a documented asset. SOPs, decision trees, pricing logic docs. If they quit tomorrow, the next person walks into clarity, not chaos. The question isn't "will they leave?" — it's "when they leave, what stays?"

🎬 **B-ROLL:** Screen recording: empty Google Drive folder or blank SOP template — visual representation of "nothing was documented." Then cut to: what the folder SHOULD look like with SOPs, pricing logic docs, decision frameworks.

⚠️ **FLAG:** Marcus was asked why he didn't require documentation during the six months. His honest answer: "I assumed a chief position would come in, and in three months I'd be presented with systems. Like hiring a magician." This is a great line — use it. But don't let it become an excuse. The video should make clear: the documentation requirement was HIS job to set, not hers to volunteer.

## 5. POINT 3 — AI as the Bridge Layer

**Framework:** AI as Institutional Memory, Not Replacement
**Timing:** 90–120 seconds

**Core Argument:**

AI doesn't fix a leadership failure. But it does close the gap between "I lost everything" and "the next hire walks into a documented system." AI is the institutional memory layer.

**What Marcus Actually Did:**

Built an AI project file with: (1) Knowledge base uploaded — frameworks on meritocracy, hiring clarity, and role expectations. (2) Current state defined — exactly where Cloud9 stands, what was lost, what's missing. (3) Goal state defined — a revenue manager performing at or above previous output, with complete documentation so the company is never naked again. (4) Constraint defined — if this person leaves in 3, 6, or 9 months, every system they touched must survive their departure.

**What AI Is Actually Doing Right Now:**

Marcus is using AI to review daily rates across 59 units. He's teaching himself to build a replacement pickup and pricing platform through code. He's using AI to prepare interview frameworks for the replacement hire. AI isn't replacing the revenue manager — it's keeping the lights on while the next system is built.

**The 30-60-90 for the Next Hire:**

30 days: Four SOPs delivered, reviewed, and clear enough that any operator could follow them. 60 days: Automation handoff — teachable processes transferred to a finance analyst. 90 days: Dashboard and AI integrations built with the dev team to scale and leverage the work.

**Application for the 5–15 Unit Operator:**

You don't need a CRO. You don't even need a full-time revenue manager on day one. You need an AI-augmented system that captures pricing logic, documents decisions, and creates assets. Then when you do hire, they walk into clarity. The AI project file becomes your institutional memory — the thing that stays when people leave.

🎬 **B-ROLL:** Screen recording of the actual AI project interface — show the prompt, the knowledge base, the conversation. Quick demo of AI reviewing rates. If comfortable: show the code being built for the pickup platform.

⚠️ **FLAG:** This section needs the most work before filming. The sprint hiring process (pitting 3–4 candidates against each other on live pricing for 10 days) is a strong concept but the evaluation methodology isn't solved yet. Marcus doesn't yet have an expert evaluator confirmed. Don't film this section until: (1) the evaluation system is defined, (2) at least one sprint cycle has been run, and (3) there's a concrete outcome to show. Otherwise this becomes theoretical — the exact thing the brand rejects.

**Timing:** 15–20 seconds
**Goal:** One synthesized takeaway. Quotable. Screenshottable.

Options — pick strongest in edit:

> "A title doesn't define a role. Documented outcomes define a role. Documented systems survive a departure. Everything else is a dependency you're pretending is a hire."

Alternate closer (darker, more Marcus):

> "I call it ignorance debt. The cost of not knowing what you don't know — and it compounds silently until someone walks out the door and you pay for it all at once."

🎬 **B-ROLL:** Tight frame on Marcus. No B-roll. Let the line land in silence. Hold for 2–3 seconds before the CTA.

## 7. CHALLENGE CTA

**Timing:** 15–20 seconds
**Goal:** Trust-building action. Not "like and subscribe." Specific enough they can't rationalize their way out.

> "Here's your move. Take your most critical hire — the one person whose departure would leave you naked. Write two lists: Tasks They Do and Systems They Build. If you can't fill out both columns with specific deliverables and deadlines — you don't have a role architecture. You have a prayer. Fix it in the next 48 hours. Because I didn't, and it cost me six months and a 30% revenue engine I couldn't keep."

🎬 **B-ROLL:** On-screen: simple two-column template — Tasks They DO | Systems They BUILD. Consider offering this as a downloadable template (future lead magnet).
