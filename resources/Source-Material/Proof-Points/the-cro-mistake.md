# The CRO Mistake — Role Architecture Failure & The "Never Naked Again" Protocol

**Date:** February 15, 2025  
**Category:** Brain Dump / Hiring & Delegation  
**Status:** Post-Mortem — Systems Rebuilt

---

## The Gap

You hired a revenue manager and called them a CRO. That's not a people problem — that's a role architecture problem.

Revenue managers optimize pricing and occupancy. CROs build revenue systems — strategy, team structure, pipeline, cross-functional alignment. You got exactly what you hired for (revenue execution), just not what you needed (revenue leadership). And because expectations were never crystallized, there was no accountability framework to catch the drift.

---

## The System — The "Never Naked Again" Hiring Protocol

### 1. Define the Outcome Before the Role

What does success look like in 90 days, 6 months, 12 months? Write it in measurable terms.

For the CRO role, that would have been:

- "Build and document a pricing strategy playbook"
- "Establish RevOps reporting cadence"
- "Own ADR optimization across all 11 units with documented SOPs"

Not just "hit revenue targets."

### 2. Separate the Skill Set from the Résumé

Revenue managers execute within systems. CROs build systems.

Before you post a role, write two columns:

- **"Tasks this person will DO"**
- **"Systems this person will BUILD"**

If the BUILD column is heavy, you need a senior operator, not a specialist.

### 3. Crystallize Before They Exit

Every person who touches your business leaves with institutional knowledge.

**Build an "Exit Extraction Protocol"** — before anyone departs, conduct a structured debrief:

- What processes did they create?
- What did they learn about the market?
- What tribal knowledge lives only in their head?

Make it a system, not a rescue mission.

### 4. Use AI as the Bridge, Not the Replacement

AI fills the gap between "I lost institutional knowledge" and "I've rebuilt it as documented systems."

The goal: next hire walks into a role with clear SOPs, defined KPIs, and AI-augmented tools so they can execute from day one instead of figuring it out.

### 5. Hire for the Specific Job with a 30-60-90

Every new hire gets a written document before their first day:

- Here's what you own
- Here's how we measure success
- Here's what good looks like at 30/60/90 days

**If you can't write that document, you're not ready to hire for that role yet.**

---

## The Proof

Every hire from this point forward walks into a system instead of a vibe. You stop losing institutional knowledge when people leave because it's already documented. AI becomes your institutional memory layer — you're never truly "naked" again even between hires.

---

## The Challenge

Before hiring anyone else, write the **"Role Architecture Doc"** for the next position:

1. **Outcomes** this role owns at 30/60/90 days
2. **Tasks vs. Systems** they're responsible for
3. **How success is measured** weekly

That document becomes your hiring filter, onboarding guide, and accountability framework all in one.
